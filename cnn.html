Aznar to urge Bush for terror list

Wednesday, May 7, 2003 Posted: 11:53 AM EDT (1553 GMT)

Bush, right, welcomes Aznar to his Texas ranch on a previous visit by the Spanish leader.

MADRID, Spain -- Spain's Prime Minister Jose Maria Aznar is expected to ask for help in trying to stamp out local rebel groups when he meets President George W. Bush in the White House Wednesday.

It is believed the prime minister will request help in ostracizing the Basque political party Batasuna by pushing for it to be added to the State Department's list of terrorist groups when the two leaders meet for a working dinner.

Aznar will argue that a worldwide list of terrorist organizations should be compiled, not just those involved with al Qaeda, Reuters news agency reports.

He made his case at the U.N. Security Council this week when he warned the attacks on the United States should not be treated as a "single and unrepeatable event."

Aznar was one of Bush's closest allies in the Iraq war, pledging Spanish troops for humanitarian efforts as well as policing and security missions. (Full Story)

The support came in the face of stiff opposition from Spaniards who held a host of mass demonstrations against the U.S.-led war on Iraq. Opinion polls showed 90 percent of the population was against the war, with Aznar's popularity plummeting. (Full Story)

He faces local elections later this month.

During his term, Aznar has moved to outlaw Batasuna for its alleged links to the armed Basque separatist group ETA which has been blamed for the deaths of 836 people since its first attack in 1968.

Spain's supreme court upheld the ban in March, after it voted 16-0 to accept the government's argument that the party could be outlawed under a law that went into effect last June, which permits the banning of parties that support terrorism. (Party banned)

Batasuna has also been accused of injuring over 2,300 people in more than 3,000 attacks, as well of sponsoring almost 4,000 acts of so-called low-level street violence since 1991.

Batasuna had denied representing ETA, but the country's top anti-terrorism judge last August suspended the party for three years and its offices were closed.

Despite Batasuna holding 10 percent of the regional vote, it came in for national criticism for not condemning the killing of a six-year-old girl in an ETA bomb attack last August that also killed a 57-year-old man.

ETA is listed a terrorist organization by the United States and the European Union.

A White House statement said: ''Spain stands among America's closest allies in the war on terrorism and the effort to bring peace and democracy to Iraq.

''Spain is a leader within Europe, a valuable member of NATO and the U.N. Security Council and an important proponent of close trans-Atlantic relations."
