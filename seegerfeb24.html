\title Hausdorff dimension, volume sets, and Fourier integral
operators
\endtitle
\abstract We prove that if the Hausdorff dimension of a subset of
${\Bbb R}^d$ is sufficiently large, then the set of volumes
determined by $k$ tuples of $E$ together with the origin has
positive Lebesgue measure. Our main tool is Sobolev bounds for the
generalized Radon transforms.
\endabstract

\enddate

Let $E \subset {\Bbb R}^d$, $d \ge 2$. A classical conjecture in
geometric measure theory, due to Falconer (\cite{Falconer86}) is
to prove that if the Hausdorff dimension of $E$ is greater than
$\frac{d}{2}$, then ${\Cal L}^1(\Delta(E))$ has positive Lebesgue
measure where ${\Cal L}^1$ denotes the one-dimensional Lebesgue
measure and $\Delta(E)=\{|x-y|: x,y \in E\}$, the set of Euclidean
distances between elements of $E$. See, for example,
\cite{Wolff02} and \cite{Mattila95} for a thorough description of
this problem and related results.

In this paper we consider the following generalization of the
distance set problem. Let $E \subset {\Bbb R}^d$. Given a
$k$-tuple $(x^1, \dots, x^k) \in E^{(k)}=E \times E \times \dots
\times E$, let $vol_{k,d}(x^1, \dots, x^k)$ denote the $k$
dimensional volume of the simplex spanned by the origin and the
vectors $(x^1, \dots, x^k)$. It is not hard to check that
$vol_{k,d}(x^1, \dots, x^k)=\frac{\sqrt{det(W \cdot W^{t}})}{k!}$,
where $W$ is the matrix $(x^1, \dots, x^k)$, and $W^t$ is the
transpose matrix. (Note that $W \cdot W^t$ is a $k \times k$
matrix). Let
$$V_{k,d}(E)=\{vol_{k,d}(x^1, \dots, x^k): x^j \in E\}. \tag0.1$$

Our goal is to determine high large the Hausdorff dimension of a
compact set $E$ needs to be before we can conclude that the
Lebesgue measure of $V_{k,d}$ is positive. Observe that the case
$k=1$ of this problem is precisely the Falconer distance problem
described above. Our main result is the following.

\proclaim{Theorem 0.1} Let $E$ be a compact subset of ${\Bbb R}^d
\ \backslash \ \{x: |x| \leq 1\}$, $d \ge 2$, of Hausdorff
dimension greater than $d-\frac{d}{k}+\frac{1}{2}$, with $k=d$ or
$k=2$. Then ${\Cal L}^1(V_{k,d}(E))>0$. \endproclaim

The Lebesgue measure of $V_{k,d}(E)$, $k \ge 2$, is not in general
positive if the Hausdorff dimension of $E$ is smaller than
$d-\frac{d}{k}$. To see this, let $\{q_i\}$ be a sequence of
positive integers such that $q_0=2$, and $q_{i+1}>q_i^i$. Let
$$ E_i=\left\{x \in {[0,1]}^d: \exists p \in {[0,q_i]}^d \cap {\Bbb
Z}^d \ \text{with} \ |x_k-p_k/q_i| \leq
q_i^{-\frac{d}{s}}\right\}, \tag0.2$$ $0<s<d$.

Let $E=\cap_i E_i$. It is known (see e.g. \cite{Falconer85}, Ch.
8) that the Hausdorff dimension of $E$ is $s$. We can cover
$V_k(E_i)$ by $\approx q_i^k$ intervals of length $\approx
q_i^{-\frac{d(k-1)}{s}}$. The former follows from the fact that
values of $vol_{k,d}(x^1, \dots, x^k)$ cover a positive proportion
of the integers between $1$ and $q_i^k$ on ${[0,q_i]}^d \cap {\Bbb
Z}^d$. The latter follows from the fact that the volume of a
$k$-dimensional simplex is bounded above by the length of the
longest side times the $(k-1)$-dimensional volume of the base. It
follows that ${\Cal L}^1(V_{k,d}(E))=0$ if $s<d-\frac{d}{k}$, as
claimed. Observe that this argument is slightly different in the
case $k=1$, that of classical distance sets. The set $V_1(E_i)$
can be covered by $\approx q_i^2$ intervals of length
$q_i^{-\frac{d}{s}}$ since the quadratic form $x_1^2+\dots+x_d^2$
takes on $\approx q_i^2$ values (up to logarithms) on the grid
${[0,q_i]}^d$, and the diameter of each "box" in $E_i$ is $\approx
q_i^{-\frac{d}{s}}$ by construction.

The aforementioned Falconer distance problem has been attacked in
two basic ways. The first approach, due to Falconer, takes
advantage of the fact that the operator $Tf(x)=\int_{S^{d-1}}
f(x-\omega) d\omega$ maps $L^2({\Bbb R}^d) \to
L^2_{\frac{d-1}{2}}({\Bbb R}^d)$, where $L^2_{\frac{d-1}{2}}({\Bbb
R}^d)$ is the $L^2$-Sobolev space consisting of $L^2$ functions
$f$ such that ${(I-\bigtriangleup)}^{\frac{d-1}{4}}f$ is also in
$L^2({\Bbb R}^d)$ in the sense of distributions. An approach later
developed by Mattila (\cite{Mattila87}), Sjolin (\cite{Sjolin93}),
Bourgain (\cite{Bourgain94}), and Wolff (\cite{Wolff99}) uses the
technology related to the regularity of the restriction operator
${\Cal R}f=\hat{f}{|}_{S^{d-1}}$. See, for example, \cite{Stein93}
and \cite{Sogge93} for a thorough background on the restriction
phenomenon and its applications. The analytic machine behind the
proof of Theorem 0.1 in this paper is the generalized Radon
transform introduced by Phong and Stein (\cite{PhongStein86}), an
operator of the form
$$ Rf(x)=\int_{\Phi(x,y)=0} f(y)dy, \tag0.4$$ where for each $x$,
$\{y: \Phi(x,y)=0\}$ is a smooth embedded hyper-surface in ${\Bbb
R}^d$. Sogge and Stein (\cite{SoggeStein90}) proved that if this
family of hyper-surfaces has non-vanishing rotational curvature,
in the sense that the determinant of the Monge-Ampere matrix
$$ M=\left( \matrix 0 & \nabla_x \Phi \\ \nabla_y \Phi &
\frac{\partial^2 \Phi}{\partial x \partial y} \endmatrix \right)
\tag0.5$$ does not equal to $0$, then $R:L^2({\Bbb R}^d) \to
L^2_{\frac{d-1}{2}}({\Bbb R}^d)$.

In the proof of Theorem 0.1, the underlying generalized Radon
transform is the operator
$$ Rf(x)=\int_{vol_{k,d}(x, y, x^3, \dots, x^k)=1} f(y)dy, \tag0.4$$
where $(x^3, \dots, x^k)$ are vectors in ${\Bbb R}^d$ which should
be viewed as parameters.

We shall see that this operator does not satisfy the rotational
curvature since the associated Monge-Ampere determinant is
identically $0$. However, we will show that there exists a
non-trivial submatrix of the Monge-Ampere with a non-zero
determinant. This will allow us to gain an appropriate number of
derivatives on $L^2({\Bbb R}^d)$ and complete the proof.

An interesting feature of the volume set problem versus the
distance set problem is the structure of the set $\{(x^1, \dots,
x^k) \in E^{(k)}: vol_{k,d}(x^1, \dots, x^k) \ge c\}$. We shall
see (Lemma 1.1 below) that the measure of this set is positive,
for a sufficiently small $c$, if the Hausdorff dimension of $E$ is
greater than $k-1$. The corresponding quantity in the distance set
problem, (i.e the case $k=1$) $\{(x,y) \in E \times E: |x-y| \ge
c\}$ trivially has positive measure for sufficiently small $c$ as
soon as the Hausdorff dimension of $E$ is positive since $|x-y|=0$
if and only if $x=y$, and singletons have $\mu \times \mu$ measure
$0$.

\vskip.125in

\head Proof of Theorem 0.1 \endhead

\vskip.125in

Cover $V(E)$ by $\cup [r_i, r_i+\epsilon_i]$. By pigeon-holing, we
may assume that $r_i \ge \frac{1}{100}$. It suffices to prove that
there is a positive constant $c>0$, possibly depending on $E$,
that
$$ \sum_i \epsilon_i \ge c. \tag1.1$$

Let $\mu$ be the Frostman measure on $E$. Let $\mu^{(k)}=\mu
\times \mu \times \dots \times \mu$. Then
$$ 1=\mu^{(k)}(E^{(k)}) \leq \sum_i \mu^{(k)}(\{(x^1, \dots, x^k)
\in E^k: r_i \leq vol_{k,d}(x^1, \dots, x^k) \leq
r_i+\epsilon_i\})$$
$$=\sum_i \int \int \int_{r_i \leq vol_{k,d}(x^1, \dots, x^k) \leq
r_i+\epsilon_i} d\mu(x^1) \dots d\mu(x^k). \tag1.2$$

It suffices to prove that the expression in $(1.2)$ is bounded by
$C \sum_i \epsilon_i$ is the Hausdorff dimension of $E$ is greater
than $d-\frac{d}{k}+\frac{1}{2}$. Let $f \ge 0$ be a measurable
function such that
$$ \int f(x)dx=1 \tag1.3$$ and
$$ \int {|\widehat{f}(\xi)|}^2 {|\xi|}^{-d+\alpha} d\xi=
\int \int {|x-y|}^{-\alpha} f(x)f(y)dxdy<\infty \tag1.4$$ for
every $\alpha$ smaller than the Hausdorff dimension of $E$.

We need the following observation.

\proclaim{Lemma 1.1} Let $E$ be a compact subset of ${\Bbb R}^d \
\backslash \ \{x: |x| \leq 1\}$ of Hausdorff dimension greater
than $k-1$. Then there exist $c_1,c_2>0$ such that
$\mu^{(k)}(\{(x^1, \dots, x^k): vol_{k,d}(x^1, \dots,
x^k)>c_1\})>c_2$. \endproclaim

We shall prove the lemma in the case $k=d$. The general case
follows in the same way by restricting to the sub-space spanned by
the $k$ vectors in question. It suffices to show that
$$ \int \dots \int \frac{d\mu(x^1) \dots d\mu(x^k)}{{|vol_{k,d}(x^1,
\dots, x^k)|}^{\beta}}<\infty \tag1.5$$ for some $\beta>0$.

Observe that $vol_{d,d}(x^1, \dots, x^d)=det(x^1, \dots, x^d)=x^1
\cdot x^2 \wedge x^3 \wedge \dots \wedge x^d$,  where $x^2 \wedge
x^3 \wedge \dots \wedge x^d$ denotes the $d$-dimensional cross
(exterior) product of the vectors $x^2, \dots, x^d$. We have
$$ \int \frac{d\mu(x^1)}{{|x^1 \cdot
(x^2 \wedge \dots \wedge x^d)|}^{\beta}} d\mu(x^1) \leq C \int
\frac{d\mu(x^1)}{{||x^2 \wedge \dots \wedge x^d|
\cos(\theta)|}^{\beta}}, \tag1.6$$ where $\theta$ is the a angle
between $x$ and $x \times z$. Let $\phi=\frac{\pi}{2}-\theta$ and
decompose the integral where $2^{-j} \leq \phi \leq 2^{-j+1}$. It
follows that $(1.6)$ is bounded by
$$ C \sum_j 2^{j\beta} {|x^2 \wedge \dots \wedge x^d|}^{-\beta}
\int_{2^{-j} \leq \phi \leq 2^{-j+1}} d\mu(x^1) \tag1.7$$ $$ \leq
C'{|x^2 \wedge \dots \wedge x^d|}^{-\beta} \sum_j 2^{j \beta}
2^{(d-1)j} 2^{-j \alpha} \leq C''{|x^2 \wedge \dots \wedge
x^d|}^{-\beta} \tag1.8$$ by choosing $\beta<\alpha-(d-1)$, since
the Hausdorff dimension of $E$ is greater than $d-1$ by
assumption, and $\mu$ is the Frostman measure. The second step in
$(1.7)$ follows from the fact that the $\mu$ measure of a set
contained in a $2^{-j}$ neighborhood of a compact subset of a
$(d-1)$-dimensional plane is at most $C2^{j((d-1)-\alpha)}$, where
$\alpha$ is the Hausdorff dimension of $E$, since this cone can be
covered by $\leq C2^{(d-1)j}$ balls of radius $2^{-j}$.

Proceeding in the same manner, we integrate with respect to $x^2,
\dots, x^d$ and complete the proof.

Using Lemma 1.1 and scaling, we see that it suffices to prove that
$$ \int \int \int_{vol_{k,d}(x^1, \dots, x^k)=1} f(x^1)\dots f(x^k)
d\mu(x^1) \dots d\mu(x^k) <\infty \tag1.9$$ provided that $(1.3)$
and $(1.4)$ hold.

Let
$$ R_{k,d}f(x)=\int_{det(x,y,x^3, \dots, x^d)=1} f(y)dy. \tag1.10$$

We have
$$ \int R_{k,d}f(x)f(x)dx=\sum_{j,k} \int
R_{k,d}f_j(x)f_{k}(x)dx, \tag1.11$$ where
$$ \hat{f}_j(\xi)=\beta(2^{-j}|\xi|)\hat{f}(\xi), \tag1.12$$ the
standard Littlewood-Paley cut-off, where $\beta$ is a smooth
partition of unity supported in $[1/2,4]$ and identically equal to
$1$ in $[1,2]$. It suffices to consider $j,k>1$, and it is also
clear by integration by parts that we need only consider the case
$|j-k| \leq 2$, since otherwise $\widehat{Rf_j}$ and
$\widehat{f}_k$ have disjoint supports. By readjusting the
definition of $\beta$ slightly, we may in fact assume that $j=k$.
We have
$$ \int R_{k,d}f_j(x)f_j(x)dx \leq {||R_{k,d}f_j||}_2 {||f_j||}_2.
\tag1.13$$

If we can show that ${||R_{k,d}f_j||}_2 \leq C2^{-\gamma_{k,d}j}
{||f_j||}_2$, where $\gamma_{d,d}=\frac{1}{2}$, and
$\gamma_{2,d}=\frac{d-1}{2}$, with constants uniform in the
parameters $x^3, \dots, x^d$, it will follow that $(1.4)$ is
bounded by
$$ C \sum_j 2^{-\gamma_{k,d}j} {||f_j||}_2^2 \leq C'
\sum_j 2^{-\gamma_{k,d}j} 2^{j(d-\alpha)} \leq C'' \tag1.14$$ if
$\alpha>d-\gamma_{k,d}$ in view of $(1.4)$.

Thus Theorem 0.1 is reduced to the following.

\proclaim{Lemma 1.2} Let $R_{k,d}f$ be as in $(1.6)$ above. Then
if $k=2$ or $k=d$,
$$ {||R_{k,d}f||}_{L^2_{\gamma_{k,d}}({\Bbb R}^d)} \leq
C{||f||}_{L^2({\Bbb R}^d)}. \tag1.15$$ \endproclaim

\demo{Proof of Lemma 1.2 in the case $k=d$} It suffices to show
that if $\Phi(x,y)=det(x,y,x^3, \dots, x^d)-1$, there exists a $3
\times 3$ sub-matrix of $(0.2)$ with a non-zero determinant as
long as $det(x,y,x^3, \dots, x^d)=1$. Indeed, the Schwartz kernel
of $R$ is supported on
$$ Z=\{(x,y): \Phi(x,y)=0\}. \tag1.16$$

Let ${\Cal C}$ denote the corresponding canonical relation:
$$ {\Cal C}=N^{*}(Z)=\{(x,\xi,y,\eta): (x,y) \in Z; (\xi, -\eta)
\perp TZ\}, \tag1.17$$ the co-normal bundle of $Z$.

The condition that there exists a $3 \times 3$ sub-matrix of
$(0.2)$ with a non-zero determinant when $det(x,y,z)=1$ is
equivalent to the statement that $\pi_x: {\Cal C} \to T^{*}X$ and
$\pi_y: {\Cal C} \rightarrow T^{*}Y$ have rank one less than the
maximum, where $T^{*}X$ and $T^{*}Y$ denote the cotangent bundle
of $X$ and $Y$ respectively. Here $X$ is the projection of $Z$
onto the $x$ variable and $Y$ is defined analogously. It then
follows from \cite{Hormander83}, Ch.XXV, that $(1.15)$ holds. See
also \cite{SoggeStein90}.

Let $A$ denote the $4$ by $4$ sub-matrix of the Monge-Ampere
matrix $M$ in $(0.3)$ with the upper left corner at $M_{11}$, and
let $A_{ij}$ denote the minor of $A$ obtained by removing the
$i$th row and $j$th column. A direct computation shows that
$$ \det(A_{22})=-g^2_{2}(x^3, \dots, x^d) \det(x,y,x^3, \dots, x^d),
\tag1.18$$
$$ \det(A_{33})=-g^2_{3}(x^3, \dots, x^d) \det(x,y,x^3, \dots, x^d),
\tag1.19$$ and
$$ \det(A_{44})=-g^2_{4}(x^3, \dots, x^d) \det(x,y,x^3, \dots, x^d),
\tag1.20$$ where $g_j$ is computed as follows. Let
$$  J=\left( \matrix x^3 \\ x^4 \\ \vdots \\ x^d \endmatrix
\right), \tag1.21$$ a $d$ by $d-2$ matrix. Let $G_j$, $j=2,3,4$,
denote the $d-2$ by $d-2$ matrix obtained by taking the $j-1$st
column along with columns $4$ through $d$.

By sliding $A$ along the diagonal of $M$ and repeating the above
procedure we can achieve the following. Let $U$ be any $d-2$ by
$d-2$ sub-matrix of $J$ created by choosing $d-2$ of the columns.
Then there exists a $3$ by $3$ sub-matrix $A_U$ of $M$ such that
$$ \det(A_U)=\det(U) \det(x,y,x^3, \dots, x^d). \tag1.22$$

We would be done if we could show that there exists a universal
constant $c>0$, depending only on the dimension, such that
$\det(U)>c$ for some $U$, a $d-2$ by $d-2$ sub-matrix of $J$.

Let
$$ L=\left( \matrix x \\ y \\ x^3 \\ x^4 \\ \vdots \\ x^d \endmatrix
\right). \tag1.23$$

Let $L_{ij}$ denote the $d-1$ by $d-1$ matrix obtained by
eliminating the $i$'th row and $j$th column of $L$. Since
$\det(L)=1$, we have
$$ \left| \sum {(-1)}^{j+1} x_j \det(L_{1j})\right|=1,
\tag1.24$$ from which it follows that
$$ |x_{j_0} \det(L_{1j_0})| \ge \frac{1}{d} \tag1.25$$ for some $1
\leq j_0 \leq d$.

Since $x,y, x^3, \dots, x^d$ are all contained in a compact set,
$|x_{j_0}|$ is bounded above by $C$, depending only on the
diameter of $E$. It follows that
$$ \det(L_{1j_0}) \ge \frac{1}{Cd}. \tag1.26$$

Let $L'=L_{1j_0}$ and define $L'_{ij}$ analogously. Since
$$ \det(L')=\left|\sum_{j \not=j_0} {(-1)}^{j} y_j L'_{1j}\right| \ge
\frac{1}{Cd}, \tag1.27$$ there exists $j_0'$ such that
$$ |y_{j_0'} \det(L'_{1j_0'})| \ge \frac{1}{Cd(d-1)}. \tag1.28$$

Again, since $|y_{j_0'}| \leq C$ due to compactness, we conclude
that
$$ |\det(L'_{1j_0'})| \ge \frac{1}{C^2d(d-1)}>0 \tag1.29$$ which
concludes the proof since $L'_{1j_0'}$ is a $d-2$ by $d-2$
sub-matrix of $J$. This completes the proof of Lemma 1.2 in the
case of $k=d$ and thus of Theorem 0.1 in the same range.

\enddemo

\vskip.125in

\demo{Proof of Lemma 1.2 in the case $k=2$} In this case
$$ \Phi(x,y)=\sqrt{{|x|}^2{|y|}^2-{(x \cdot y)}^2}, \tag1.30$$
however, it is more convenient (and quite sufficient) to work with
$$ \Phi(x,y)={|x|}^2{|y|}^2-{(x \cdot y)}^2. \tag1.31$$

A calculation shows that the Monge-Ampere determinant equals
$$ c_d \Phi^2(x,y) {(x \cdot y)}^{d-2}. \tag1.32$$

We would be done if we could show that there exist $c_1,c_2>0$
such that $\mu \times \mu (\{(x,y) \in E \times E: |x \cdot y|
 >c_1\})>c_2$. This follows immediately from the proof of Lemma 1.1
above. This completes the proof of Lemma 1.2 in the case $k=2$,
and thus of Theorem 0.1 in the same range.

\enddemo
