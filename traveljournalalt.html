July 15, 2005

My European trip is almost over and it has been a good one! Mathematics went extremely well, I met some interesting people, and I finally made the decision to make the case that MathEd is an intellectually bankrupt discipline that is contributing to the deterioration of the mathematics education in the US. The time has come to do something about it. I am incredibly gratified by the positive response I have received from so many people about my MathEd web page. As I suspected, my feelings on the matter are shared very widely. The main source of skepticism is whether one can actually do anything about it. I am determined to prove that one can.

Administrative thinking is so difficult to overcome! Once an academic discipline has been established and starts bringing in grant money, it seemingly does not even occur to administrators to question whether it has any validity as an intellectual enterprise. I am convinced that if alchemy came back in style and started bringing in outside funding, most administrators would not even flinch. Keep in mind that alchemy was actually useful in that many of the techniques of modern chemistry were developed in the process of trying to turn lead into gold and similar harebrained schemes. MathEd does not even have that advantage for they contribute nothing to our understanding of anything except stupidity, greed and career driven thinking.

A question I sometimes hear is that given that several big time mathematicians are involved with MathEd, how could this operation be so worthless... Well, the number of serious mathematicians involved in this bulls*** is so small, a general explanation may not be required. I suspect, however, that big time mathematicians involved in MathEd may not be aware of what actually happens on the ground, so to speak. If one of those guys showed up at Mizzou and watched our master MathEd practitioner invert 1+x in nilpotent rings using the Axiom of Choice, he or she might think twice about associating with this collection of half-wits and losers. As I keep saying over and over, it is not the humor of the situation that is most striking, for these nincompoops are not that funny. The point is that they are actually f***ing over students of all levels and this should not be allowed to continue!

May 25-28, 2005

I am enclosing a (heavily edited) version of reports I sent to my friends from the K-8 Mathematics Education conference at Asilomar.

Day 1:

I just set through the first session of the Math Knowledge conference at Asilomar. The main subject of discussion was how to divide 27 by 6. Several "creative" interpretations were offered and "correct" answers ranged from 4 to 5 to 4.5. By the end of the discussion I was no longer sure how to perform this calculation.

The food is remarkably bad here. Dinner consisted of overcooked salmon with herbs of unknown origin, very bland spare ribs, strangely colored black beans, and corn on the cob. Unfortunately, the nearest restaurant is, as far as I can tell, miles away, so I will lose a few pounds in the next few days. Unfortunately, the nearest bar is also very far away and I have not yet figured out how to deal with this calamity.

During the brief group discussion segment of the program, I had the pleasure of interacting with two elementary school teachers from California. The experience left me deeply fearful of the future and seriously contemplating home schooling. I was bombarded with questions like, "what does it mean to understand mathematics?" or "what is the difference between a concept and a problem?"... Lord protect us...

Day 2:

When I tell people I know that I just had an amazing day, they tend to point out that this is not unusual for me. Nevertheless, today was quite interesting. As I mentioned earlier, I participated in a panel discussion on proofs in elementary school. The participants were asked to come up with a visual model illustrating the fact that if n divides m, and p divides n, then p divides m. Well, I drew an n by l array of squares and did some obvious things from there. The mini group I was a
part of consisted of an elementary school teacher, sitting to my right, and a graph theorist sitting to my left. I was supposed to explain my model to those individuals. The elementary school teacher did not understand the model for quite a while, and when she finally did, insisted that much preparatory work must be done before third graders have any chance at all of grasping these "deep ideas". The graph theorist to my left was deeply disturbed by this, as was I...

Our second task was to read a "case study", a five page document describing what happened in a certain third grade classroom where this divisibility issue was discussed. We were given twenty minutes to read and absorb. When I finished my reading in five minutes, I was told by one of the moderators that I may want to read the document again lest I missed something... After twenty minutes were up, the moderators apologized for the time constraints and said that it was perfectly
understandable that many people were still reading. We then proceeded to discuss the document, which resulted in a protracted argument about rules of solitaire.

At dinner I set with two secretaries from the MSRI who are basically running the conference. They asked me what I think of the whole thing and I told them that this is a little bit like visiting the St. Louis Zoo, except that these monkeys get to mess with young children on a daily basis...

I have not yet pissed anybody off, as far as I can tell. I was even very patient with the elementary school teacher who did not understand little squares modeling multiplication. I tried to explain to her, very politely, that if she does not mess with the kids and just lets them think, they may not grow up to be total dumb asses... I told her that what she calls "preparatory concepts" is actually a set of boring and often misleading constraints that prevent children from thinking, even when they are perfectly capable of solving the problems at hand. I got an impression that she sort of understood what I was saying.

In any case, I have had enough of this ****. Tomorrow morning I am going whale watching, and tonight I am going drinking. Life is too short for overdoses of MathEd!!!

Day 2 had more thrills in store for me... I was on the evening panel that was asked to answer the following four questions:

(1) What is an integer?

(2) What does it mean to add two integers?

(3) What does it mean to subtract two integers?

(4) Why is a-b=a+(-b)?

I tried to make up the most ridiculous explanations possible because, quite frankly, I thought it was time to piss somebody off... I came up with the infinite trash can scheme where subtraction is done by moving things from the right hand side of the table into the "infinite trash can" and then adding things to the left hand side of the table from the "infinite trash can", if necessary. At least two of the elementary school teachers present loved the idea and seemed to have no clue that I was making fun of the whole process. They also seemed to be under the impression that I have been refining this scheme for years and did not believe my claim that I made it up on the fly...

It is late at night, and I am easily amused anyways, but the idea that the NSF, not to mention the department, is spending money to send me here is cracking me up and keeping me awake. What a ****ing waste! One of the secretaries told me that the budget for this conference is $145,000 !!! Can you ******* believe it?! There are people starving on the streets of Oakland, about 100 miles away, and we are sitting here, munching on (poorly prepared) salmon filets and discussing what are integers...
