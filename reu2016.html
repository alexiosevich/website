REU 2016

Organizers: Alex Iosevich (iosevich@math.rochester.edu) and Jonathan Pakianathan (jonpak@math.rochester.edu) with the assistance of Brendan Murphy and Qiaofeng Zhu.

This summer, Jonathan Pakianathan and I are running an informal research program for Rochester undergraduate students on pseudo-random graphs. We shall begin by reading a seminal paper by Krivelevich and Sudakov, enclosed below. We shall then examine a variety of graphs that came up in our recent investigations of Erdos type problems in vector spaces over finite fields. Connections between pseudo-random graphs and topics in data science are also going to be explored.

We are going to meet twice a week, on Tuesdays and Wednesdays, 2-4 p.m.. During the first two weeks, we are also going to meet on Mondays in order to accelerate the learning of the background material. The first couple of lectures will be dedicated to the basic facts about graphs. The next couple of lectures will deal with random graphs. Once we have this background under control, we shall go through the paper by Krivelevich and Sudakov and then move on to applications.

For some background on basic graph theory you can take a look at a variety of texts and lecture notes. A particularly nice recent set of notes is by Benny Sudakov, enclosed below. To get a nice introduction to the theory of random graphs, the participants are encouraged to take a look at the original paper by Erdos and Renyi where this notion was first introduced.


Literature:

Graph Theory, by Benny Sudakov

On random graphs I, by P. Erdos and A. Renyi

Pseudo-random graphs, by Krivelevich and Sudakov

Pseudo-random graphs web page, by Fan Chung

Finite analogs of Euclidean space, by Medrano, Meyers, Stark and Terras

Fourier bases: an elementary viewpoint on a variety of applications, by Iosevich, Mayeli and Senger (incomplete)



And here are a few useful links that are not explicitly about graph theory but cover topics and techniques relevant to this program.

Incidence theorems and their applications, by Zeev Dvir

Erdos distance problem in vector spaces over finite fields, by A. Iosevich and M. Rudnev

On the distribution of distances in homogeneous compact metric spaces, by M. Herman and J. Pakianathan

A note on the unit distance problem for planar configurations with Q-independent direction set, by M. Herman and J. Pakianathan

The number of distances is almost linear for most norms, by J. Matousek





Rough plan: To paraphrase Mike Tyson, a former boxing champion, everybody has a plan until they get smacked in the nose. Nevertheless, here is a rough plan for the next few weeks. We are going to begin by going over basic graph theory, along the lines of Benny Sudakov's notes enclosed above, with a few wrinkles thrown in. We shall then give a quick crash course on elementary probability, along the lines of the last part of the second volume of Apostol's Calculus, followed by a gentle introduction to random graphs. Once we have these notions under control, we shall move on to pseudo-random graphs, guided by the aforementioned paper by Krivelevich and Sudakov. In the research phase of the program, we are going to consider some naturally arising pseudo-random graphs that come from geometric combinatorial problems in vector spaces over finite fields.
