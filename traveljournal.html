January 16-22. 2006

The visit to England was wonderful, as always. The first night I stayed with Misha and Masha in London. We had dinner at a Georgian restaurant Tbilisi! The food was wonderful, as was Cha-cha!

The following day we went to Bristol where I gave a talk and we then proceeded to work on our finite field stuff and related things. We are making really nice progress! I have never been happier with mathematics. As usual, an amusing moment came... During the trip back to London, I decided that it is possible to realize a field with p^(n-1) elements as a subfield of a field with p^n elements, and used this idea to construct counter-examples to everything Misha and I have recently done. Fortunately, we eventually realized that the argument is ridiculous... since n/(n-1) is only an integer if n=2... Embarassing but funny...

January 6-11, 2006

My remaining days in Iraklion were equally pleasant and interesting. On Sunday, an earthquake woke me up as I was taking a nap in the afternoon. I bounced a few times, then sat down in bed and bounced a few more times. I then dressed and left the building, but the earthquake was over by then. An amusing expeirence, though a little scary at first. The strength of the quake was about 6.9 at the source, but large populated areas were not effected at all. I suspect that a 6.9 earthquake in Athens would bring about a disaster of historic proportions. It is my impression that building codes are either non-existent or at least not followed in this country. Moreover, the layout of Athens would make large scale rescue operations very difficult. I am not even talking about Iraklion. Trying to send a dozen ambulances from the hospital near the university to the downtown area would be about as easy as reaching the moon...

My last night in Iraklion, the Admiral (Themis Mitsis) and Marisa took me out for dinner and we had a wonderful time. It was really good to see all these people. I will miss them very much in Missouri!

September 13-25, 2005

The trip to Armenia was extremely educational in some ways. The harmonic analysis conference I attended took place in Izikadzor, a resort town high in the mountains. Water was typically turned off at night, so if I happened to go to the bathroom between the hours of 11:00 p.m. and 7:00 a.m., I used cleaning pads for my computer screen, of which I fortunately had many. The internet connection was largely disfunctional and seemed to fail precisely at the moments when I was about to send an email.

The conference itself was quite good, with many excellent lectures and productive discussions afterwards. Armenian mathematics is going through a difficult period right now, with few current journal or books coming in. However, many strong mathematicians and mathematics students remain, and there is every reason to believe that in time the situation will get much better.

In the middle of the conference, an excursion to several ancient local monestaries was organized. The architecture and the surrounding scenerey was absolutely breathtaking. I will post several pictures from the excursion on this web page in the next few days.

There one story I absolutely cannot resist telling. On the way out of Armenia I flew to Amstermdam on Aramavia airlines. About fifteen minutes before the flight landed in Amsterdam, several people got up, took their luggage out of overhead compartments, and started moving towards the door. Flight attendants made a feeble attempt to out an end to this process, but to no avail! One of the passangers brushed my head with his suitcase as he stumbled towards the door as the plane was headed towards the landing, and looked at me rather angrily for getting in his way... An amazing trip!

June 25, 2005

Making plans is nice, but reality always takes over. When I arrived in Cherbourg, the attendant at the train station told me that to get to the Utah beach, I need to take a train to Caen and then take a bus. When I arrived in Caen, I was told that I need to take a train back to Bayueux and go from there. In Bayueux I found a room in a hotel for 19 Euros and a five hour tour of D-Day sites for 37 Euros. I got back from the tour about an hour ago and it was absolutely wonderful. We went to a D-Day museum, a German artillery bunker, the Omaha beach, the American military cemetery at the Omaha beach, and Point du Hoc where American rangers scaled the cliffs and destroyed large German artillery pieces that endangered both the Utah beach and Omaha beach landings. The Omaha beach visit was especially haunting. It looks so peaceful now, but 863 Americans died there in just a few hours. There were over 4000 American casualties at the Omaha beach overall, including the wounded and the captured.

This is slightly off the subject, but I wish to assure everyone that during my tour today, I did not see a single whale. Not one...

I strolled around downtown Bayueux and found La Fringale, a wonderful little restaurant with outdoor seating. This where I am now. I am sipping on some semi-dry white wine, and waiting for a bowl of French onion soup, followed by a fillet of sole. Heaven is located in Bayueux this evening!

Tomorrow I will get up when I get up, and I will take a train to Orleans to visit Philippe Jaming for a week. If I get up early, I may try to visit Rouen, but I suspect that I will sleep in.

June 15-19, 2005

On Wednesday morning I left St. Louis, and after a stop in Cincinnati, where I bought a copy of the Da Vinci Code, I boarded an Air France flight to Paris. The person sitting next to me was a plump Austrian exchange student named Melinda. In the first ten minutes of our conversation she informed me that she is uncomfortable around Turks and that there are lots of them where she is from. My interest in any further discussions quickly waned and I proceeded to concentrate on the Da Vinci Code, an unforgivable 400-page exercise in murder of trees, though mildly entertaining. The fact that the Vatican heavily criticized the Da Vinci Code created a moral imperative for me to buy the book and read it, and I do not regret the experience even though the novel is rather shallow and of dubious historical veracity.

After landing in Paris, I immediately experienced the wonders of being in France. Charles de Gaulle airport is a sprawling disorganized disaster where directions are poorly marked and the information staff is incompetent, even in their own language. I eventually figured out where to buy tickets for Cherbourg and took off. Since I was saving Bristol money by taking the train, I bought first class tickets and enjoyed a very pleasant ride through the scenic French countryside. In the early afternoon I arrived in Cherbourg where I got a hotel room and slept very soundly through the rest of the afternoon and early evening, with periodic breaks to read the Da Vinci Code. I finally got out of bed at about 8:00 p.m. and took a nice stroll around the city. I found a place to check my email and later settled on a cute little restaurant next to the plaza with a big statue of Napoleon on a horse. The meal was outstanding. I had several beers and some veal with Provencal sauce and noodles. The waitress was not rude, by French standards, so I left a nice tip, also by French standards.

On the morning of June 17 I took a ferry to Poole, England. I almost missed the ferry because the idiots at Charles de Gaulle failed to stamp my passport, so the customs officials at the ferry port were wondering how exactly I entered the country. I had five minutes to board the ferry, so I pretended not to understand French, and since they did not speak any English (f******* customs officials in Cherbourg on the f****** English Channel!) they just let me go…  How did that country last this long?! Yeah, I know… periodic American assistance, for which they are deeply ungrateful What are you going to do…

After arriving in Poole, I walked over to the train station and boarded a train to Bristol. I had to switch trains in Southamton. My connecting train was late and the announcements were misleading outright stupid. I expect more from England, but European influence is difficult to shake off. Well, that is not entirely fair. German trains still run mostly on time as far as I know.

When I arrived in Bristol I learned that Michael Rudnev is getting tenure after an initial decision to fire him. I guess there are occasional limits to butt ugly with head up your a** type stupidity. We celebrated wildly and will continue to do so for several days, I suspect.

One of the professors at Bristol asked me if I believe that GW is the worst President in American history. May 17-22, 2005

My trip to New York was a blast! It was great to see Mel, Sinai, Izabella, Julia, Sinan, Jozsef, and many many others. The lectures were excellent, except mine, of course, and meals, especially Korean BBQ, were absolutely outstanding.

It is always a pleasure to interact with people who have a very similar sense of humor to mine. During one of the lunches it came out that John Steinberger from UC Davis is originally from Switzerland. This prompted Sinai Robins to immediately ask: "... doesn't he owe us money? ...".

Another wonderful development was the decision by Sinai, Izabella and I to launch a new journal named EJAC- Electronic Journal of Analytic Combinatorics. Our plan is to initially run it as a web based journal from Mizzou, and then sign up with a publisher. It was pointed out that once the journal goes off-line, it will go from EJAC to JAC-OFF... We will have to see what happens.

Julia and I decided to write an undergraduate textbook on the Erdos distance problem. It will be partly based on the notes we are currently writing for the Mizzou summer program for high school students the two of us are running in August. I am really excited about this project.

 

April 26, 2005

The trip to Kansas State was quite amusing in many ways, though the drive was hopelessly boring. It takes about three hours and fifteen minutes to get there, no matter how much you speed. If the traffic in KC does not get you, something else will.

The Department of Mathematics at KSU is a remarkably quiet place. I saw a total of about fifteen people during my stay, including the people I ran into in the hallways. Before my colloquium talk I had a very nice buffet style lunch with Andrew Bennett, Robert Burckel, Marianne Korten, Pietro Poggi-Corradini, and Dimitry Ryabogin. The discussion was mostly about politics of various sort, culminating in a discussion of PBS, the Middle East, and President Abbas' holocaust denying doctoral dissertation in Moscow.

The colloquium talk seems to have gone well. I talked mostly about the relationship between the Falconer and Erdos distance conjectures, the Erdos integer distance principle, and applications to analysis. Robert Burckel, Marianne Korten, and Pietro Poggi-Corradini asked some interesting questions and I felt that the most of the audience was paying attention at least to some extent.

The colloquium dinner later that evening was very very nice. Marianne Korten, her daughter, a friend, Robert Burckel and Dimitry Ryabogin took me to a very nice local restaurant specializing in cuisine of uncertain origin. I had a clam chowder followed by steak, accompanied by several beers from a local brewery. The discussion was much more tame this time around, mostly about my April 1 pranks and similar activities. After dinner I went straight to sleep as I was extremely tired. A great visit though!

The following morning I had lunch with Dimitry and drove back to Columbia, listening to Beethoven's sonatas over and over. I played the Pathetique at several recitals when I was in high school and developed and intense emotional relationship with the piece. I can literally listen to it four or five times in a row and be equally excited each time.

My next trip is probably to New York City on May 17 to speak at the conference in honor of Mel Nathanson's 60th birthday. I especially look forward to Korean BBQ, and, of course, to playing basketball in Mel's back yard!
